Marathon Man

Notes for Historians at the Movies

Eric Rauchway

Marathon Man was a Paramount Pictures movie of 1976, produced by Robert Evans and Sidney Beckerman and directed by John Schlesinger from a script by William Goldman based on his 1974 novel of the same name. It comes from a time in movie history familiar to Historians at the Movies; it starred Dustin Hoffman, who was in #HATM-covered All the President’s Men the same year, and Roy Scheider, who was in #HATM-covered Jaws the previous year. It garnered Laurence Olivier an Oscar nomination for Actor in a Supporting Role; that award went to Jason Robards for All the President’s Men. As it happens, All the President’s Men also pulled in an Oscar for adapted screenplay for William Goldman—his second; he had won a best original screenplay Oscar for Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid, which might someday qualify for #HATM treatment. And I bet a lot of folks will be familiar with Goldman’s other great success, with the 1973 book and the screenplay for The Princess Bride (1987); moreover, his World War II epic, A Bridge Too Far (1977) might also someday belong on the #HATM list. He was a great talent and he had range.

If you want to put the movie in context, the poster for Marathon Man calls it a thriller, which it obviously is, and you can think of it as a movie that belongs in conversation with other 1970s thrillers that pit an ambitious Hollywood leading man against a shadowy government conspiracy. So you might think of Robert Redford in Three Days of the Condor (1975) or Warren Beatty in The Parallax View (1974). I bet Hoffman was thinking of those, anyway. You might also think of it as connected to other gritty New York City movies of the same era; it comes from the same year as Taxi Driver and just after The Taking of Pelham One Two Three (1974)—the latter of which is, for my money, the best New York City movie of all time. Marathon Man came out the year before the blackout and the “Son of Sam” murders. It’s to some degree in a mythical New York City—this movie is very much suffused with the myth of Kitty Genovese, which was taken as truth by many New Yorkers at the time, that nobody responds to a call for help. (Although Marathon Man does have a twist on that, when the neighborhood delinquents do respond to Hoffman’s actual telephone call for help, but let’s not get ahead of ourselves.) But it has to be New York City, because of the need for history to be present, in the form of communities of German and Jewish émigrés from Europe.

For me, though, I think it’s most useful to think of this story as a very personal story for Goldman, and one that’s suffused with trauma, both individual and social. So yes, as Charlie Pierce already led the way in pointing out, a lot of you may want a warning that there’s A Scene in this movie. And indeed there’s a lot of content people might want to be warned about: lethal violence, suicide, torture, plus a certain amount of thoughtless racism and sexism.

But I think The Scene stands in for much bigger traumas that are central to the movie, and churning away underneath it. Goldman was a Jewish writer whose father committed suicide, so it’s not a big stretch to suppose that when he writes about a Jewish writer whose father committed suicide, there are some personal stakes. The movie is also drenched in the generational trauma of Jewish Americans of that era, and indeed since: the Holocaust.

All that trauma, both individual and social, is invoked in this film, and also, importantly I think, unresolved.

Goldman was fascinated and frustrated by history, too; that was how he got into writing about Butch Cassidy, he was just taken with the story and also how difficult it was to really know anything about it. Uncertainty was one of his big themes. You may know his maxim about Hollywood: “nobody knows anything.” But it’s often misquoted, with the emphasis on “anything”; the emphasis belongs on “knows”: nobody is certain of anything; “not one person in the entire motion picture field knows for a certainty what’s going to work.” He also thought that nobody really knows any one, either. And that’s what Goldman understood about history, too. We don’t know much; we can’t know much. But, as he said about writing Butch Cassidy: “I did all the research I could bear,” and then started writing. The script took him four weeks. But he had researched for eight years. You have to do as much research as you can endure—and then “once you start writing, go like hell” but, he added, in a phrase that is also evocative of Marathon Man, “don’t fire till you’re ready.” Writing history is an act first of endurance, and then violence. But also, it’s futile; you can do the best job possible with the narrative and still not make it come out right: “endings, frankly, are a bitch.1

And even if you do get it right, it might not make you feel good about it. It won’t relieve your suffering; it won’t expunge your trauma.

The central metaphor of the movie is endurance; as Goldman says, how much can you bear. That’s what a marathon is about—endurance. The movie opens with footage of Ethiopian runner Abebe Bikila at the 1964 Olympics, winning the marathon for the second time; in 1960, he had won running barefoot. He exemplified endurance, and the recurring image of him in the movie exemplifies endurance as well.

Hoffman’s character, Babe Levy, sets out to master history, to vindicate his father, to write the narrative that will make everything write. But, as his (thoroughly obnoxious) mentor warns him, as his brother warns him, he can’t do that. While history can destroy you—look what it does to the poor old woman in the diamond district—you can’t defeat it. You can only learn to endure the pain that the past inflicts on you.
